## What this rubric measures

This rubric evaluates the user's ability to identify and clearly
communicate a sensory need to another person — a teacher,
colleague, stranger, or friend — and to advocate for an
accommodation or adjustment in a way that is likely to be
understood and acted upon.

PEERS provides the foundational skills here: trading information
(Session 1), two-way conversation (Session 2), and peer entry
strategies (Session 6). The sensory advocacy context extends
these skills to a domain that is especially important for autistic
individuals and largely absent from mainstream social skills
training.

Critically, this rubric does not evaluate whether the user
*should* need a sensory accommodation — that is a given. It
evaluates how the user communicates that need in a way that
is clear, non-apologetic, and effective. Following Milton
(2012), the framing of sensory needs as deficits to be hidden
is rejected. Feedback supports confident self-advocacy.

## Scoring criteria

### Score 1 — The need is not communicated or is communicated
in a way likely to create confusion or conflict

The other person would not know what is needed, or the turn
is likely to create defensiveness rather than a helpful response.

**Examples:**
- Staying silent and becoming visibly distressed while the
  person remains unaware.
- "This is unbearable. Can you do something?" (expresses
  distress but does not name the need or what action is
  required)

**What is missing:** Any identification of the specific sensory
issue and any indication of what would help.

### Score 2 — The need is named but the request is unclear or
heavily apologetic

The sensory issue is identified but the request for accommodation
is unclear, or the user over-apologises in a way that may
make the other person unsure whether an action is actually
being requested.

**Examples:**
- "I'm sorry, I know it's probably just me, but the music
  is a bit loud, maybe?" (names the issue but excessive
  hedging and apology make it unclear whether action is
  being requested)
- "I have sensory issues." (names the category but does not
  say what is needed right now)

**What is missing:** A clear, direct request for a specific
accommodation. The other person needs to know what to do.

### Score 3 — The need and the request are both clear

The user names the sensory issue and makes a clear, specific
request. The communication is functional and the other person
can act on it.

**Examples:**
- "The music is quite loud for me — could you turn it down
  a little, please?"
- "I need a quieter spot to work. Is there somewhere I can
  move to?"

**What is present:** Named sensory issue, specific and
actionable request, polite framing.

### Score 4 — Clear, confident, and appropriately framed

The user advocates clearly and without excessive apology.
The phrasing is confident without being demanding. There
may be a brief explanation that helps the other person
understand the context, making them more likely to respond
helpfully.

**Examples:**
- "I'm quite sensitive to noise — could we turn the music
  down? It would really help me concentrate."
- "I need to let you know that I find crowded spaces
  overwhelming. Could we find a quieter place to talk?"

**What is present:** Clear identification of need, confident
(non-apologetic) phrasing, a brief explanation that invites
understanding rather than demanding compliance.

### Score 5 — Confident, clear, and contextually skilled

The user advocates effectively and in a way that is likely to
result in the accommodation being provided. The phrasing
is warm, clear, and positions the need as legitimate without
excessive explanation. The user may offer an alternative if
the first request cannot be met.

**Examples:**
- "I have a sensory need around loud environments — I work
  best somewhere quieter. Could we use the meeting room,
  or is there another option?"
- "The background noise is making it hard for me to focus
  right now. Could we either move somewhere quieter or
  come back to this in a few minutes?"

**What is present:** Everything in Score 4, plus either an
alternative being offered or a framing that makes the request
feel collaborative rather than one-directional.

## Feedback framing principles

- **Sensory needs are legitimate, not inconvenient.** Feedback
  must never frame the sensory need itself as a problem to
  be minimised — only offer ways to communicate it more
  effectively.

- **Confidence is a skill, not a personality trait.** If a user
  over-apologises, frame the feedback as: "you could drop
  the 'sorry' here — your need is real and you don't need
  to apologise for having it. A direct request is more likely
  to get a helpful response."

- **Milton (2012) principle:** The difficulty of sensory
  advocacy is partly a social context problem — many
  environments are not designed with sensory diversity
  in mind. Feedback should acknowledge this: "it's a
  genuinely difficult ask to make in a loud environment,
  and you handled it well by naming exactly what you need."

- **Never evaluate the sensory need.** Whether the noise
  level seems loud, whether the request seems reasonable
  to a neurotypical observer — these are irrelevant. The
  rubric evaluates communication effectiveness only.

- **Over-apologising and under-requesting are equally worth
  noting.** Both make it harder to get the accommodation.
  Frame both as practical communication choices, not
  personality judgments.

## What the critic must never say

- Never imply the user should have managed the sensory
  difficulty themselves rather than asking for accommodation.
- Never say the request was "too much to ask."
- Never suggest the user should apologise more, or that
  their request came across as demanding, without also
  acknowledging that confident advocacy is appropriate.
- Never frame the sensory need as a disorder or problem
  the user has — use "sensory need" or "sensory sensitivity,"
  not "condition" or "issue with."
- Never reward excessive self-deprecation. A turn that
  scores higher because the user apologised more is not
  progress — it is the opposite.
