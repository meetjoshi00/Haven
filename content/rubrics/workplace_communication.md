## What this rubric measures

This rubric evaluates the user's ability to communicate
effectively in structured, higher-stakes environments —
classrooms, offices, job interviews, group projects. Skills
assessed include: making requests of authority figures, asking
for deadline extensions, giving and receiving feedback,
introducing oneself professionally, and joining or contributing
to group work.

Grounded in PEERS skills for trading information (Session 1),
two-way conversation (Session 2), electronic communication
(Session 3), planning get-togethers/collaborative work
(Session 8), and resolving arguments (Session 12).

Workplace and school contexts require register flexibility —
the ability to adjust formality to the setting. This is a
learnable, strategic skill, not a test of personality. Following
Milton (2012), autistic individuals often have excellent
capacity for clear, direct, logical communication that is
highly valued in many professional contexts. Feedback should
build on this strength while developing contextual register
awareness.

## Scoring criteria

### Score 1 — The communication is likely to create a
significant obstacle in this context

The turn would likely result in the request being refused,
misunderstood, or the relationship being damaged in a
professional setting.

**Examples:**
- "I can't do this by Friday. The deadline is unrealistic."
  (states the problem but no request, no acknowledgment
  of the teacher/manager's position, likely to create
  defensiveness)
- "I don't really know what this job involves." (appropriate
  thought, catastrophic context for a job interview)

**What is missing:** Any acknowledgment of the other
person's position or context, or the turn is at a register
that is inappropriate for the setting (too casual, too
confrontational, or too self-undermining).

### Score 2 — The request or contribution is there but the
register is off or key elements are missing

The user's intent is clear but the framing is likely to
create friction or fail to achieve the goal in this context.

**Examples:**
- "Can I have more time on the assignment?" (request
  present but no reason given, no acknowledgment of
  impact on the teacher, no suggested alternative deadline)
- "Yeah I'll join the project I guess." (agreement present
  but passive framing may be read as reluctant or
  uncommitted in a professional setting)

**What is missing:** Context or reason for the request,
appropriate register for the setting, or the turn lacks the
elements that make a professional request credible and
easy to agree to.

### Score 3 — Functional and appropriately formal

The turn is correctly register-matched for a professional
or academic setting, makes the request or contribution
clearly, and includes enough context for the other person
to respond helpfully.

**Examples:**
- "I'm finding it difficult to complete the assignment by
  Friday — would it be possible to have until Monday?"
- "Hi, I'm [name]. I'd be happy to join the project —
  what do you need from me?"

**What is present:** Appropriate register, clear request
or contribution, enough context for the other person to act.

### Score 4 — Professionally effective and relationship-aware

The turn is well-formed, appropriately formal, and shows
awareness of the other person's needs and context. It is
likely to result in a positive outcome and preserve or
build the professional relationship.

**Examples:**
- "I wanted to ask about the Friday deadline — I've been
  making progress but I'm concerned I won't be able to
  produce my best work in time. Would it be possible to
  extend to Monday? I can have a draft to you by Friday
  if that would help."
- "I've reviewed the project brief and I think I can
  contribute most in the research section. Is that still
  available, or would another area be more useful to
  the team?"

**What is present:** Appropriate register, clear request,
acknowledgment of the other person's context, a
constructive element (offering a draft, identifying a
contribution) that makes the request easier to accept.

### Score 5 — Professionally skilled and contextually fluent

The turn demonstrates strong professional communication:
clear, appropriately formal, considerate of the other
person's perspective, and structured in a way that makes
the desired outcome highly likely. The user may anticipate
potential objections or offer alternatives proactively.

**Examples:**
- "I'd like to discuss the Friday deadline if you have
  a moment. I'm making good progress but I want to
  make sure the quality is where it needs to be. I could
  have a complete draft to you by Friday and the
  final version by Monday — would that work for you?"
- "Thank you for considering me for this role. I've
  prepared some questions about the work — could you
  tell me more about how the team collaborates day
  to day? I find I work best when I understand the
  structure clearly."

**What is present:** Everything in Score 4, plus either
proactive alternatives, considered questions, or a
framing that shows genuine understanding of the other
person's position and makes the interaction feel
collaborative rather than one-directional.

## Feedback framing principles

- **Direct, logical communication is a professional strength.**
  Autistic communication is often clearer and more precise
  than neurotypical communication in professional contexts.
  Feedback should acknowledge this: "your request is very
  clear — in a formal setting, adding one sentence of
  context makes it even easier for the teacher to say yes."

- **Register adjustment is a strategic tool, not a
  personality change.** Frame formal language as a context-
  specific choice: "in a workplace, slightly more formal
  phrasing tends to get requests taken more seriously —
  this isn't about being less yourself, it's a tool for
  this context."

- **Milton (2012) principle:** Professional environments
  often have implicit social norms that are rarely explained
  explicitly. Autistic individuals are disadvantaged not
  because they lack ability but because these norms are
  unwritten. Feedback should make the norm explicit:
  "in most workplaces, requests for extensions are more
  likely to be granted when a reason is given, even briefly.
  This is an unwritten rule worth knowing."

- **Acknowledge when the setting is inherently difficult.**
  Job interviews and formal requests of authority figures
  are stressful for everyone. Difficulty 3 workplace
  scenarios should be acknowledged as genuinely
  challenging.

- **Precision is an asset.** Where the user has been very
  precise or specific, name this as a strength — it is
  often what makes a professional request credible.

## What the critic must never say

- Never imply the user should be less direct or less precise
  — only help them add context or adjust register.
- Never suggest that formal language means "being fake"
  — frame it as context-appropriate tool use.
- Never penalise the user for asking questions or for
  wanting information — curiosity and precision are
  professional assets.
- Never frame a request as "demanding" if the content
  is reasonable — evaluate phrasing only.
- Never imply the user should accept unreasonable
  conditions without advocating for themselves — the
  goal is effective advocacy, not compliance.
