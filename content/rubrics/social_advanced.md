## What this rubric measures

This rubric evaluates the user's ability to navigate higher-stakes
social situations: politely disagreeing, handling teasing or conflict,
declining requests, and managing the emotional register of a
conversation. It maps to PEERS skills for "handling teasing,"
"resolving arguments," "handling bullying and bad reputations,"
and "handling rumors and gossip" (Laugeson & Frankel,
Sessions 10–13).

At difficulty 3, personas are realistic — some may push back,
show mild impatience, or not respond warmly. This mirrors the
real-world social environment autistic individuals often navigate.
Feedback at this level focuses on strategic communication choices,
not on conformity to a single norm.

Following Milton (2012), the double empathy problem is most
visible in high-stakes social encounters — the disjuncture between
differently disposed social actors becomes more marked when
emotions are involved. Feedback should acknowledge this
complexity, not flatten it.

## Scoring criteria

### Score 1 — Escalates or closes the interaction

The turn makes the situation worse, significantly escalates
tension, or completely shuts down the possibility of
productive exchange.

**Examples:**
- "You're completely wrong and that's a stupid thing to say."
  (personal attack, no engagement with the content of the
  disagreement)
- Silence / no response / walking away mid-conversation
  without any acknowledgment.

**What is missing:** Any acknowledgment of the other person's
position before asserting a different one. The turn treats the
interaction as a contest rather than an exchange.

### Score 2 — Position stated but delivered in a way likely to increase friction

The user's position or need is clear but the framing is likely
to increase resistance rather than invite understanding.

**Examples:**
- "That's not what I said. You're not listening to me."
  (corrects the person but attributes negative intent)
- "I don't want to do that." (declines but gives no context
  and offers no alternative, likely to leave the other person
  confused or offended)

**What is missing:** Acknowledgment of the other person's
position before asserting one's own, or a brief explanation
that helps the other person understand the refusal or
disagreement.

### Score 3 — Handles the situation without making it worse

The user manages the situation functionally. They state their
position or decline clearly, do not escalate, and the interaction
can continue or end without lasting tension.

**Examples:**
- "I see what you mean, but I actually think it works
  differently. Can I explain?"
- "I'd rather not do that, thanks." (clear, not rude, low
  escalation risk)

**What is present:** Clear statement of position or decline,
no personal attack, the other person can understand the
user's stance even if they disagree with it.

### Score 4 — Handles the situation constructively

The user manages the disagreement or difficult moment in a
way that keeps the door open. They acknowledge the other
person, assert their own position clearly, and invite
continued exchange or close the conversation warmly.

**Examples:**
- "I understand where you're coming from, but I see it
  differently — I think [reason]. Does that make sense?"
- "I appreciate you asking, but that doesn't really work
  for me right now. Maybe another time."

**What is present:** Acknowledgment of the other person's
position, clear assertion of own position or need, an
invitation to continue the exchange or a warm close.

### Score 5 — Handles the situation with both clarity and genuine social skill

The user manages a difficult social moment in a way that
feels natural, warm, and grounded. They hold their
position clearly without aggression, show they have heard
the other person, and navigate the exchange in a way that
is likely to preserve or even strengthen the relationship.

**Examples:**
- "That's a fair point actually — I hadn't thought about
  it that way. I still think [position] because [reason], but
  I get where you're coming from."
- "I really appreciate you asking me. I can't this time,
  but I'd genuinely like to do something else with you
  another time — would [alternative] work?"

**What is present:** Genuine acknowledgment (not just
performative), clear and confident position, an element
that maintains or restores the relationship.

## Feedback framing principles

- **Difficulty 3 situations are genuinely hard for everyone.**
  Acknowledge this before offering a suggestion: "That
  kind of pushback is tricky for most people."

- **The goal is strategic awareness, not emotional
  suppression.** Autistic individuals are often told to mask
  or suppress direct communication. Feedback here should
  develop strategic options, not suggest the user's directness
  is wrong.

- **Milton (2012) principle at this level:** In conflict or
  disagreement situations, the double empathy problem is
  most acute. The user is not failing to empathise — they
  are navigating a situation where both parties may struggle
  to understand each other. Frame feedback as: "one way to
  help the other person understand your position is..."

- **Distinguish between what worked and what created
  friction.** High-stakes turns often have elements of both.
  Name both before suggesting anything.

- **Never evaluate emotional authenticity.** If a user
  expresses frustration, the feedback must not imply they
  should not feel frustrated — only that the phrasing might
  affect the outcome they want.

## What the critic must never say

- Never say the user "overreacted" — evaluate the phrasing
  and its likely effect, not the underlying emotion.
- Never imply that asserting one's position is wrong —
  only offer ways to assert it that are likely to achieve
  the user's goal.
- Never tell the user to "be more understanding" of the
  other person without also acknowledging what was
  difficult about the situation.
- Never compare the user's response to "the mature" or
  "the right" way to handle it.
- Never frame avoidance or silence as a worse option
  than engagement without acknowledging that sometimes
  stepping away is the right call.
