## What this rubric measures

This rubric evaluates the clarity, politeness, and conversational
reciprocity of a user's turn in everyday social interactions —
ordering food, making requests of strangers, greeting people,
and similar low-stakes encounters. It is grounded in PEERS
skills for "trading information," "two-way conversation," and
"making polite requests" (Laugeson & Frankel, Sessions 1–2).

The goal is not to make autistic users sound neurotypical.
Following Milton (2012), communication difficulty in social
situations is a mutual problem — it arises from a mismatch
between two differently disposed people, not from a deficit
in one person. Feedback trains contextual awareness and
strategic flexibility, not conformity to a single communication
norm.

## Scoring criteria

### Score 1 — Very hard for the other person to respond helpfully

The turn makes it difficult for the interaction to continue
productively. The other person would likely be confused,
offended, or unable to provide what is needed.

**Examples:**
- "Coffee." (single word, no greeting, no please, no size)
- "Why is this taking so long? I just want something to eat."
  (opens with complaint unrelated to the request)

**What is missing:** A minimal request structure (what you
want + please), or the turn begins with an unrelated negative
that blocks the interaction before it starts.

### Score 2 — The request is there but something important is missing

The core request is present but lacks either politeness markers,
necessary detail, or acknowledgment of the other person. The
interaction can continue but may require extra effort from
the other person.

**Examples:**
- "I want a medium latte." (request present, no please or
  greeting)
- "Um, can I get, I don't know, maybe a coffee or something?"
  (request present but so vague the other person cannot act
  on it without asking several follow-up questions)

**What is missing:** A politeness marker (please/thank you),
or enough detail for the other person to act on the request
without needing to ask multiple follow-up questions.

### Score 3 — Functional — gets the job done

The request is clear, polite, and contains enough information
for the other person to respond. It may feel slightly flat or
transactional but would work well in most contexts.

**Examples:**
- "Can I have a medium latte please?"
- "I'd like a black coffee, medium size, please."

**What is present:** A clear request, at least one politeness
marker, sufficient detail. The interaction can complete
smoothly from here.

### Score 4 — Good — clear, polite, and appropriately warm

The turn is well-formed, polite, and shows some social
awareness — perhaps a brief greeting, an acknowledgment
of the context, or a small courtesy that makes the exchange
feel easy for both people.

**Examples:**
- "Hi, could I please get a medium oat milk latte?"
- "Excuse me, I'd like a black coffee, medium size please —
  thank you."

**What is present:** Everything in Score 3, plus at least one
element that acknowledges the other person (greeting,
excuse me, thank you timed appropriately).

### Score 5 — Excellent — warm, clear, and contextually aware

The turn is socially fluent for the context. It communicates
clearly, shows awareness of the other person's situation
(e.g. they are busy, other people are waiting), and completes
the request in a way that makes the interaction easy and
pleasant for both parties.

**Examples:**
- "Hi! Could I get a medium oat milk latte when you have
  a moment? No sugar, thank you so much."
- "Excuse me — medium black coffee please, whenever
  you're ready. Thank you!"

**What is present:** Everything in Score 4, plus a brief
acknowledgment of context or a warm sign-off that makes
the interaction feel complete and considerate.

## Feedback framing principles

- **Frame feedback around what the other person experiences,
  not what the user did wrong.** Instead of "you were rude,"
  say "a barista who's very busy might find it hard to know
  what you need from that phrasing."

- **Acknowledge what worked before noting what could
  shift.** Every turn has something that is going in the right
  direction — name it first.

- **Describe the context, not the person.** "In a busy setting,
  adding a please helps the interaction go smoothly" is
  better than "you need to be more polite."

- **Never imply the user's natural style is wrong in all
  contexts.** Some communication styles are direct and
  efficient — they work well in some situations. The coaching
  helps the user understand when a different approach helps
  them get what they need.

- **Milton (2012) principle:** The communication gap is
  mutual. Feedback should help the user navigate contexts
  where neurotypical norms dominate, not suggest their
  natural style is defective. Phrase it as: "in this context,
  adding X tends to make things go more smoothly."

## What the critic must never say

- Never say "that was wrong" without immediately offering
  what would work better in its place.
- Never imply the user's natural communication style is
  a problem that needs fixing.
- Never use the word "rude" — instead describe the
  effect: "a busy barista might struggle to know what
  you need."
- Never compare the user's response to "how most people"
  or "normal people" would say it.
- Never give feedback that implies the persona's reaction
  is the only valid one — other people might respond
  differently.
